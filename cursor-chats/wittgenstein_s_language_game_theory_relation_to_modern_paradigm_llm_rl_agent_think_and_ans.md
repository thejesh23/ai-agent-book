### 1. Early Wittgenstein: The Picture Theory of "Language is the World"

Wittgenstein's early thought is concentrated in his work *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*. Its core can be summarized as the "Picture Theory of Language," which attempts to draw a clear and rigorous logical boundary between language, thought, and the world.

#### Core Ideas:

*   **The World Consists of "Facts"**: Wittgenstein begins his work: "The world is the totality of facts, not of things." He argues that the basic units of the world are not objects (like tables or chairs), but "states of affairs" or "atomic facts"—the simplest combinations of relationships between objects.

*   **Language is a "Picture" of the World**: He believes that meaningful linguistic propositions are "logical pictures" of reality. Just as a map can depict the actual structure of a geographical area, a meaningful sentence must correspond in its logical structure to the "fact" it describes. Words in a sentence correspond to objects in a fact, and the way words are combined (syntactic structure) must reflect the way objects are combined in reality.

*   **"The limits of my language mean the limits of my world"**: This is one of the most famous statements in the *Tractatus*. Wittgenstein argues that anything that can be clearly and logically spoken about belongs to the knowable scope of this world. For areas that cannot be clearly depicted with logical language, such as ethics, aesthetics, religion, and metaphysics, language loses its function. Because they do not correspond to any "fact," they cannot form valid "pictures."

*   **The Conclusion of Silence**: Hence, his famous conclusion: "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent." This is not a denial of the value of ethics or aesthetics, but rather a belief that they lie beyond the boundaries of what language can clearly express. The only legitimate function of language is to depict and state facts.

In summary, **the early "language is the world" theory is a strict essentialist and logicist thought. It assumes a unified, universal logical structure behind language, which is isomorphic to the inner structure of the world. The essential function of language is limited to "depicting reality."**

### 2. Later Wittgenstein: The Tool Theory of "Language Games"

After years of philosophical reflection, Wittgenstein completely overturned his early ideas. His later philosophy is concentrated in *Philosophical Investigations*. He abandoned the attempt to find a single essence of language and instead turned to studying the diverse uses of language in specific situations.

#### Core Ideas:

*   **"Meaning is Use"**: This is the cornerstone of his later thought. Wittgenstein no longer believes that the meaning of a word comes from the object it refers to or its intrinsic essence, but from its specific "use" in a particular context. The meaning of a word is the role it plays in a "language game."

*   **"Language Games"**: He compares the various uses of language to different "games." Giving orders, making reports, telling jokes, praying, greeting, guessing riddles—these are all different language games. Each game has its own implicit rules, goals, and participants. Like a chess piece, the meaning of a "pawn" is not determined by its material, but by its movement and rules within the "game" of chess. In different board games, the same piece might have a completely different "meaning."

*   **Language is a "Toolbox"**: To illustrate further, he compares language to a toolbox. It contains hammers, screwdrivers, saws, glue, etc. These tools have different shapes and functions. We don't ask, "What is the essence of a tool?" but rather, "What is this tool used for?" Similarly, we should not ask, "What is the essential meaning of a word?" but instead observe and understand how it is used in different linguistic activities.

*   **"Form of Life"**: Language games do not exist in a vacuum; they are deeply rooted in human social, cultural activities, and shared patterns of behavior, which Wittgenstein calls "forms of life." For example, the language game of "apologizing" can only exist because, within the "form of life" of human social interaction, there is a set of interrelated behaviors and expectations involving making mistakes, asking for forgiveness, and receiving pardon. The meaning of language is inseparable from the cultural and life background in which it is embedded.

In summary, **the later "language game" theory is a pragmatic and functionalist thought. It no longer seeks a single essence of language but instead views language as a collection of "tools" with different functions, used by humans in various "forms of life."**

### 3. The Relationship: A Philosophical Revolution from Essentialism to Pragmatism

The relationship between Wittgenstein's early and later thought is not a simple evolution or supplement, but a profound **self-critique and philosophical revolution**. The later thought is a complete subversion of the earlier one.

1.  **From Unity to Diversity**: The early theory sought a single, unified logical essence (depicting facts) behind all language. The later theory demonstrates the infinite diversity of language use, arguing that limiting language's function to "depiction" is a huge mistake. This is as absurd as believing that the only function of every tool in a toolbox is to "hammer nails."

2.  **From Rigidity to Flexibility**: The early theory depicted a language world as pure as crystal, with a fixed logical structure. The later theory compares language to an ancient city: "a maze of little streets and squares, of old and new houses, and of houses with additions from various periods." Language is dynamic, changing, and even somewhat messy; its rules are conventional, not a priori logical laws.

3.  **From the Boundaries of "World" to the Practice of "Life"**: The early thought used the boundaries of logical language to define the boundaries of the "world." The later thought shows that our "world" is woven together by the countless language games and "forms of life" we participate in. Our way of understanding the world is not through a single logical lens, but through our ability to master and use language in various social practices.

**Conclusion**:

It can be said that Wittgenstein's early philosophy is a classic example of the "philosophical disease" he himself later criticized—that is, philosophers always trying to find a single, eternal essence for complex and diverse phenomena. His philosophical journey was from constructing a grand, rigorous logical system to ultimately recognizing the futility of this effort, and instead embracing the richness, complexity, and fluidity of language in the practical life of human beings.Therefore, "language is the world" represents an idealized, essentialist pursuit of language, while "language games" represent a pragmatic, contextualized understanding of language. The relationship between the two is the most famous instance of "self-negation" in the history of philosophy, and it also marks a major shift in analytic philosophy from logical positivism to the ordinary language school.
